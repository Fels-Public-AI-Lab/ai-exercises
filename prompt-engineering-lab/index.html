Background

When you type a message to an AI assistant, that message is called a prompt. The same underlying model can produce wildly different results depending on how you phrase your request — because the prompt is essentially everything the AI has to work with.

Think of it like giving directions. "Go somewhere nice for dinner" and "Find an Italian restaurant within a 10-minute walk that's open on Sunday and has outdoor seating" will lead you to very different places. AI tools work the same way: vague input produces generic output; specific, contextualized input produces something actually useful.

This skill — deliberately crafting your input to get better outputs — is called prompt engineering. Despite the technical-sounding name, it's fundamentally a communication skill. You don't need to know how AI works under the hood to do it well. You just need to practice thinking carefully about what you actually want and how to communicate that clearly.

In this exercise, you'll take a deliberately bad starting prompt and improve it through four rounds of revision, comparing the results as you go. By the end, you'll have developed your own intuitions about what makes a prompt work.

Add Role & Audience

~10 min

Why This Works

AI tools respond to context. When you tell the model who you are, who will read the output, and what the purpose is, it can calibrate vocabulary, depth, and emphasis accordingly. A briefing for a city council member looks different from a memo for a technical team — the AI can adjust, but only if you tell it to.

Action

Rewrite your prompt using the template below. Replace every placeholder (shown in yellow) with your own information. Then run it in the same AI tool you used in Task 1.

Prompt Template — Version 2 of 4
I am a [YOUR ROLE — e.g., "graduate student", "nonprofit coordinator", "policy analyst"] working on a project about [YOUR ISSUE]. I need a concise overview of this topic for [YOUR AUDIENCE — e.g., "a general audience unfamiliar with the topic", "a city council member", "my classmates in a policy course"]. My goal is to [YOUR PURPOSE — e.g., "understand the main causes and current responses", "identify the two or three biggest policy debates in this area"].

Add Structure & Constraints

~10 min

Why This Works

Specifying format, length, and specific questions to answer reduces the model's guesswork. Instead of deciding what counts as a "good answer," it follows your structure. This also makes the output easier to use — you get what you actually need, organized the way you need it.

Action

Build on your Version 2 prompt by adding the structural elements shown below. You can copy your Version 2 prompt and add to it, or use this template as a starting point. Customize the three questions to match what you actually want to know about your issue.

Prompt Template — Version 3 of 4
[Paste your Version 2 prompt here, then add the following:] Please structure your response to answer these three questions: 1. [QUESTION 1 — e.g., "What are the root causes of this problem?"] 2. [QUESTION 2 — e.g., "What policy solutions have been tried, and with what results?"] 3. [QUESTION 3 — e.g., "What are the main points of disagreement among policymakers or experts?"] Use clear headings for each section. Keep your total response under 400 words. Write for someone who is informed but not a specialist in this field — avoid jargon.

4

Add Guardrails

~10 min

Why This Works

So far you've told the AI what to do. Now try telling it what not to do. Negative constraints — sometimes called guardrails — help prevent common failure modes: overconfident claims, unverifiable statistics, unsolicited opinions, or inappropriate framing. They're especially important in professional contexts where accuracy and neutrality matter.

Action

Add the guardrails section below to your Version 3 prompt. Choose the constraints that make the most sense for your issue and your use case. You can customize the language or add your own constraints.

Prompt Template — Version 4 of 4
[Paste your Version 3 prompt here, then add the following:] Important constraints: - Do not cite specific statistics unless you are confident they are accurate. If data is relevant but uncertain, describe the general trend instead. - Do not make policy recommendations or express a political opinion. - If something is genuinely contested or debated, present it as such rather than stating one view as fact. - [ADD YOUR OWN — e.g., "Do not use acronyms without first spelling them out" or "Focus only on the United States context"]

R

Reflection

~20 min — answer all five

Heads Up

These questions ask for your thinking — not a summary of what the AI did. Answers that simply describe the outputs without interpretation or personal connection will not demonstrate the learning this exercise is designed to develop. Aim for 3–5 sentences per question.

Reflection 1

Looking across all four versions, which single change made the biggest difference in output quality? Why do you think that particular change had such a strong effect?

Think about what information you gave the AI that it didn't have before, and how the model would "use" that information differently.

Reflection 2

Was your Version 4 output actually usable — could you hand it to someone as-is? What would still need human editing, verification, or judgment before it could be used professionally?

Be honest and specific. What did the AI get right? What did it get wrong, miss, or oversimplify?

Reflection 3

Think about a task you do regularly — at work, in school, or in your personal life. How could a well-crafted prompt help you with it? Write a draft of that prompt in your answer.

This should be something real and specific to you, not a hypothetical. The goal is to connect what you practiced here to something you'll actually use.

Reflection 4

A classmate argues: "AI just does what you tell it — so a better prompt is just better instructions. There's nothing unique about working with AI compared to, say, writing a good Google search." How would you respond based on what you experienced today?

Where is your classmate right? Where do you think they're missing something? What makes prompting different from a search query?

Reflection 5

In your view, what is the most important thing a professional (in any field) should understand about prompt engineering before using AI tools at work? Frame your answer as advice to a colleague who has never used AI before.

Draw from your experience in this exercise, but think beyond it. What's the principle you'd most want someone to internalize?
