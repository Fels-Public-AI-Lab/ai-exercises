Background

Most people use AI the same way they use a search engine: type a question, read the answer, move on. But AI assistants are capable of something search engines cannot do — they can hold a conversation. You can follow up, push back, ask for clarification, change direction, or build on a previous answer. This is called a multi-turn conversation, and it's often where AI becomes genuinely useful rather than just convenient.

The catch is that a multi-turn conversation requires planning. What do you want to know? In what order should you ask? When should you accept an answer and when should you push for more depth? When should you change course? These are design decisions — and making them intentionally is the difference between a conversation that produces something valuable and one that just produces a lot of text.

This exercise puts you in the role of a researcher. You'll choose a question relevant to your field or interests, design a conversation strategy before you start, conduct the conversation, and then critically evaluate what you got. The goal is not just to use AI effectively — it's to know when the output is good enough to use and when it isn't.

0
Choose Your Research Question
⏳ ~5 min

Your research question should be specific enough to actually answer in a focused conversation, but substantive enough to require more than one exchange. It should be genuinely related to your field, career, or interests — not a random topic you just chose for the exercise. Good research questions are specific, contested or complex (not simple fact-lookups), and actionable.

Examples — Good vs. Weak Questions

Weak questions (avoid these):

  • "What is climate change?" — too broad; can be answered in one sentence
  • "What is the capital of France?" — single-fact lookup, not research

Stronger questions (aim for this):

  • "What evidence exists that community-based reentry programs reduce recidivism, and what are the conditions that make them effective?"
  • "How have healthcare systems in other high-income countries managed to control drug costs, and what barriers exist to applying those approaches in the U.S.?"
  • "What are the most effective evidence-based approaches for adult workforce reskilling in manufacturing-heavy regions?"
1
Plan Your Conversation
⏳ ~10 min
Action

Before you open your AI tool, plan the conversation you're going to have. A good multi-turn conversation is structured — you have an opening question, follow-up questions that dig deeper, and an idea of what you'll do if the AI goes off-track or gives a vague answer.

Conversation Design Tips
  • Open broad, then narrow. Start with your main question, then follow up with requests for more detail, specific examples, or alternative perspectives.
  • Ask for evidence. Don't accept "studies show" — ask which studies, from what years, with what findings.
  • Challenge vague answers. If the AI says "it depends," ask what it depends on. If it says "many experts agree," ask which experts and what the disagreement is.
  • Use the previous answer. Reference what the AI said: "You mentioned X — can you explain that in more detail?" This is more efficient than starting over.
  • Know your stopping point. Decide in advance what a satisfying answer looks like so you know when to stop.

2
Conduct the Conversation
⏳ ~25 min
Action — Important Requirements
  • Open a new conversation in your AI tool (don't use a previous conversation)
  • Your conversation must include at least 6 exchanges (6 prompts from you, 6 responses from the AI)
  • Follow your plan from Task 1, but feel free to adapt as the conversation develops
  • If the AI gives a vague or unsatisfying answer, push back — don't just move on
  • At the end, ask the AI to summarize its key findings in a brief paragraph
Staying on Track

Signs you need to redirect the conversation:

  • The AI keeps repeating the same general points without adding depth
  • The AI gives you confident-sounding statements without any supporting evidence
  • The answers are getting longer but not more informative
  • The AI is drifting away from your actual research question

When this happens, try: "Can you be more specific about [X]?" or "Can you give me a concrete example of that?" or "You've mentioned this a few times — is there a reason this is particularly important?"

3
Evaluate the Output
⏳ ~15 min
Action

Score the AI's overall output using the rubric below. For each criterion, choose a score (1–4) and write a brief justification based on what you actually observed in the conversation.

Criterion What to look for Score (1–4) Your justification
Accuracy Are specific claims, statistics, dates, and names plausible and verifiable? Did you spot any obvious errors, exaggerations, or things you'd want to verify?
Depth & Nuance Did the AI go beyond surface-level summaries? Did it acknowledge complexity, tradeoffs, or contested evidence? Or did it oversimplify?
Relevance & Focus Did the AI stay on topic and answer your actual research question? Or did it drift into general background information that wasn't specifically what you needed?
Usability Could you actually use this output in a real project — cite it, build on it, present it? Or would significant revision, verification, or augmentation be required?

4
Improve the Weakest Section
⏳ ~10 min
Action

Identify the part of the AI's output that received your lowest rubric score, or the section you found least satisfying. Use a targeted follow-up conversation to improve it. This could mean pushing for more specificity, asking for evidence, requesting a different framing, or asking the AI to reconsider something it said.

Improvement prompt template
Looking back at our conversation, I found your response about [TOPIC/SECTION] to be [DESCRIBE THE WEAKNESS — e.g., "too vague," "lacking specific evidence," "oversimplified," "missing the nuance around X"]. Can you revisit that section and provide a more [SPECIFIC IMPROVEMENT — e.g., "evidence-based," "specific," "nuanced"] response? Specifically, I'd like you to [CONCRETE REQUEST — e.g., "cite at least two specific studies or programs," "acknowledge the key counterarguments," "focus on the U.S. context only"].

R
Reflection
⏳ ~20 min — 5 questions
Before You Write

These reflection questions ask about your experience and judgment — not a summary of the AI's output. Strong answers will be specific, honest, and connected to what you observed firsthand. Answers that are vague, generic, or primarily about the AI rather than your own thinking will not demonstrate the learning this exercise is designed to build.

Reflection 1 of 5
Looking back at your conversation plan vs. what actually happened: how much did you deviate, and why? What does that tell you about the challenge of planning a conversation with AI in advance?
Think about whether the AI's answers shaped your questions, or whether your questions drove the conversation.
Reflection 2 of 5
What was the most useful thing the AI contributed to your research? What was the least useful? Be specific — point to actual moments in the conversation.
Avoid vague answers like "it was helpful overall." Identify specific exchanges or specific claims that were or weren't useful.
Reflection 3 of 5
Your rubric gave the AI a total score of —/16. In plain language: would you use this output in a real project? If yes, what would you need to do to it first? If no, what would you do instead?
"Use it" means something different in different contexts — a class paper vs. a professional deliverable vs. a personal decision. Be specific about your context.
Reflection 4 of 5
This exercise asked you to push back, ask follow-up questions, and probe for depth. How often do you actually do that when using AI in your daily life or studies? What would it take to make that a habit?
Be honest about your actual habits — not what you think you should be doing.

Draw on Exercises 1–4. What patterns have emerged across all your AI interactions?
