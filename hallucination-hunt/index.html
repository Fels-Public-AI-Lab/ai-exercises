Background: Why AI Hallucinates

Large language models generate text by predicting what words should come next based on patterns in their training data. They do not "look things up" the way a search engine does — they produce plausible-sounding text based on what has appeared in similar contexts. This means they can be confidently, fluently wrong.

This phenomenon is called hallucination — when an AI produces information that sounds convincing but is fabricated, outdated, distorted, or just incorrect. It's not lying in the human sense; the model has no intent. It simply generates what fits the pattern, whether or not it corresponds to reality. Statistics, names, dates, citations, and specific details are particularly prone to hallucination.

The most important skill for using AI responsibly is not knowing what it can do — it's knowing when to trust it and when to verify. This exercise is designed to build exactly that instinct. You'll generate a substantive AI output on a topic you personally know well, then systematically fact-check what it tells you. By the end, you'll have a much clearer sense of where AI tends to go wrong and how to respond when it does.

Generate the Output
~10 min
Action

Open a fresh conversation in your AI tool. Use the prompt template below to get a substantive output about something specific within your domain. You want detailed, specific content — the kind where inaccuracies would be detectable.

Prompt Template — Task 1
I'd like you to write a detailed overview of [SPECIFIC TOPIC WITHIN YOUR DOMAIN — e.g., "the history of zoning laws in Philadelphia", "how cognitive behavioral therapy works for anxiety disorders", "the lifecycle of a federal grant application"]. Please include: - Key facts, figures, dates, or statistics where relevant - Specific names of people, organizations, laws, or programs - How this topic has evolved or changed over time - Any current debates, challenges, or open questions Write approximately 400–500 words. Be as specific and detailed as possible.
After Running

2
Extract and Classify Claims
~20 min
Why This Matters

Not every sentence in an AI output is a factual claim. Some statements are general descriptions, logical inferences, or hedged opinions. For this task, focus on specific, verifiable claims — numbers, dates, names, attributions, program descriptions, historical events. These are where hallucinations tend to hide.

Confront the AI
~10 min
Action

Pick one specific error or inaccuracy from your fact-check. Return to your AI conversation and tell it what it got wrong. Use the prompt template below. Screenshot how it responds.

Prompt Template — Task 3

4
Reflect on the Pattern
~10 min
Action

Before moving to the reflection questions, take a moment to review your fact-check table holistically. Look at the types of errors you found — not just whether they were wrong, but how they were wrong.

R
Reflection
~20 min  ·  5 questions
Important

These questions ask for your analysis and judgment — not a replay of what happened. Strong answers will go beyond describing what the AI did and explain what it means for how you should use AI tools going forward.

Question 1 of 5
What percentage of the AI's specific claims were accurate? Does that number — whatever it is — surprise you? Why or why not?
Think about your prior expectations of AI reliability. Where did they come from? Were they based on direct experience or assumption?
Question 2 of 5
Think about someone in your field who is not AI-savvy — a colleague, a supervisor, a client. If they used AI to get information about your domain and didn't fact-check it, what would be the consequences? Be specific.
Think about actual decisions that might be made based on this kind of information, and what could go wrong.
Question 3 of 5
What made the errors hard (or easy) to catch? Was it the specificity of the language, the plausibility of the claim, or something else?
This is about the form of AI errors, not just their presence. What makes a hallucination convincing?
Question 4 of 5
When the AI was corrected (Task 3), how did it respond? What does that interaction reveal about how AI "knows" things — and why that's fundamentally different from how a human expert would respond to being corrected?
Consider what it means to learn from a correction vs. simply generate a different response.
Question 5 of 5
Based on this exercise, describe two specific rules you'll personally follow when using AI for research or information-gathering in the future. Be concrete — not "I'll be careful," but actual practices.
Think about what checks you could realistically build into your workflow. Consider both the domain and the type of claim.
