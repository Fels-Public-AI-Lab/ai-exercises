The Framing Audit

Testing how context and framing shape AI responses

⏳ ~65 minutes 🔬 Hands-on 📷 3 screenshots ✍️ Typed reflections ⚖️ No right answers
Learning Objectives
  1. Observe how changing framing, context, or demographic details affects AI outputs
  2. Analyze patterns in AI responses across different framings
  3. Understand why AI models may reflect societal biases
  4. Apply this knowledge to responsible professional use of AI tools

How This Exercise Works

🆕 Choose a scenario, then run the same scenario three times with different framing details — recording each response.
📋 Use the comparison table to analyze differences across all three outputs systematically.
📷 Take a screenshot after each AI run (3 total). Label each one as instructed in the exercise.
💡 Answer five reflection questions drawing on your observations. There are no correct answers — analytical depth is what matters.
Tool-agnostic: Use any AI assistant — ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, Copilot, or another tool of your choice. All prompts are copy-ready.
BG
Background
Read before starting

AI models learn from enormous amounts of human-generated text — including text that reflects the biases, assumptions, and patterns of the societies that produced it. When you ask an AI a question, the answer you get isn't coming from a neutral information machine. It's shaped by what kinds of writing appeared in the training data, how often certain groups were portrayed in certain ways, and whose perspectives were most represented.

This doesn't mean AI is always biased in obvious ways, or that every output is problematic. But it does mean that the same situation described with different details — different names, demographics, or contexts — can produce meaningfully different responses. The word framing refers to the details and context that surround a request. This exercise will help you see firsthand how framing affects what AI tells you, and what that means for real-world use.

One clarification: this exercise isn't about catching AI doing something wrong. The goal is careful observation. You'll run the same basic scenario three times with different framings, compare the results systematically, and draw your own conclusions. What matters is not whether AI is "good" or "bad," but whether you understand how it behaves — and can account for that when you use it.

Choose Your Scenario
~5 min

Select a scenario you'll use throughout this exercise. Each scenario involves writing something about or for a person. You'll run the same scenario three times, changing specific framing details each time. The scenario itself stays the same; the framing — who the person is, what context is described — is what changes.

Note

You are not testing whether the AI is "racist" or "sexist" in an obvious way. You're looking for subtler patterns: differences in tone, word choice, length, what gets emphasized, what gets left out, and what assumptions are embedded in the response.

R
Reflection
~20 min — 5 questions
Before You Write

These questions require you to analyze and interpret — not just describe. There are no correct answers, but strong responses will cite specific observations from your outputs, go beyond surface differences, and connect your findings to real-world implications.

Question 1 of 5
What differences did you find most significant? Not just what was different, but why does that difference matter in a real-world context where this document might be acted on?
Think about what a hiring manager, supervisor, teacher, or lender might do differently based on what you observed.
Question 2 of 5
One explanation for what you observed is that AI simply mirrors patterns in its training data — which reflects society's existing biases. Another explanation is that you introduced the biases yourself by choosing how to frame the prompt. How do you weigh these two explanations?
Both can be true simultaneously. The interesting question is: what does each explanation imply for how to use AI responsibly?
Question 3 of 5
Imagine you work at an organization considering using AI to help draft performance reviews, loan decisions, or student assessments. Based on what you observed in this exercise, what would you want your organization to think through before implementing that?
Think about stakeholders, accountability, consistency, and what gets lost when framing isn't controlled.
Question 4 of 5
Did any of the AI's outputs surprise you — either by being more neutral than expected, or more different than expected? What does that tell you about your prior assumptions about AI bias?
Question 5 of 5
What would you tell a colleague who says, "I don't worry about AI bias because I always give it the same prompt"? What's missing from that reasoning?
Think about what "the same prompt" means when the real-world situation involves different people.
